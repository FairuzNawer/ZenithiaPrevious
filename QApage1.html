Ask A Psychologist

I’m in my mid-30s and I’ve been struggling with overwhelming feelings of failure. I’ve always been a high achiever in both my career and personal life, but lately, I feel like I’m falling short of my own expectations. I constantly compare myself to others, and no matter what I accomplish, it feels like it’s never enough. It’s starting to affect my confidence, and I’m finding it hard to stay motivated. I’m worried that this is leading to depression, but I’m not sure. How do I break out of this negative cycle and regain my sense of self-worth?

  • Dr. Karen Mitchell
  • Sep 20, 2024

I’m sorry to hear you’re struggling with these feelings. It’s common for high achievers to put immense pressure on themselves, and what you’re experiencing sounds like it could be rooted in perfectionism and self-comparison, which can be exhausting. One thing to remember is that everyone’s journey is unique. What might help is to start practicing self-compassion—acknowledge your achievements, but also be kind to yourself when things don’t go as planned. It could be beneficial to set more flexible goals and focus on the progress you’re making rather than comparing yourself to others. Therapy or even mindfulness exercises can also help you break the cycle of negative thoughts and restore your confidence.

I’ve been married for over ten years, and while my partner and I have always had a strong relationship, I’ve noticed that we’ve been arguing more lately, and we seem to be drifting apart emotionally. It’s not that we’re fighting about big issues, but it’s the small, everyday disagreements that are wearing us down. I’m worried that if we don’t address these problems, they’ll build up and cause real damage to our relationship. How can we reconnect and improve communication before things get worse?

  • Dr. James Porter
  • Aug 22, 2024

It’s great that you’re recognizing these shifts in your relationship and wanting to address them before they become bigger issues. The small disagreements you mention are often signals of unmet needs or feelings that aren’t being fully communicated. I’d suggest scheduling a time when you and your partner can have an open, honest conversation about how you’re feeling. It can help to use “I” statements to avoid blame, such as “I feel distant when we argue about X.” Couples therapy can also be a safe space for both of you to express your concerns and learn how to reconnect. In the meantime, small gestures of affection and quality time together can help re-establish your emotional bond.

Over the past few months, I’ve been feeling emotionally numb. I used to be an optimistic person who felt joy in the little things, but now everything feels flat. Even things that used to excite me, like spending time with friends or working on my hobbies, don’t seem to have the same effect anymore. I haven’t gone through any major life changes, so I don’t understand why I’m feeling this way. Could this be a form of depression, and if so, how do I start feeling engaged with life again?

  • Dr. Emily Parker
  • Sep 2, 2024

Feeling emotionally numb can be disheartening, especially when you don’t know what’s causing it. It’s possible that what you’re describing could be a form of depression, known as anhedonia, where people lose interest in things that once brought them joy. This feeling can stem from burnout, stress, or even underlying emotional struggles. Taking small steps toward re-engaging in activities—even if they don’t feel as fulfilling right now—can help jump-start your emotional response. Reaching out to a therapist can also help you uncover what might be contributing to this sense of numbness and guide you toward strategies for regaining your emotional balance. It’s important to be patient with yourself as you work through this.

I’ve been dealing with chronic anxiety for most of my adult life, and while I’ve learned to manage it through therapy and medication, I still find myself getting triggered in social situations. I’ll be fine for a while, but then something small will happen, like someone making a joke at my expense, and I’ll spiral into overthinking, worrying about how I’m perceived by others. It’s exhausting, and it makes it hard to form deep connections with people. I want to stop feeling so on edge and insecure in social settings, but I don’t know how. Are there any coping mechanisms that might help me feel more comfortable and confident around others?

  • Dr. Sarah Johnson
  • July 15, 2024

It’s understandable that social situations can feel overwhelming when you have anxiety, especially if you’ve been managing it for a long time. You’ve made great strides by going to therapy and taking medication, and now it’s about finding strategies that can help in the moment. Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) techniques can be very effective for social anxiety—by challenging negative thoughts and replacing them with more balanced ones. Grounding exercises or breathing techniques can also help you stay present and calm when you feel triggered. Practicing self-affirmations before social situations might boost your confidence too. Over time, these small changes can help you feel more at ease around others.
